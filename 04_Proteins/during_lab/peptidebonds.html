Monomer → Dimer: Dehydration & Hydrolysis

Hands‑On Guidance Using Glycine & Alanine Models

Reminder You already built glycine and alanine models. Today you will join two monomers into a dipeptide (dimer) using a dehydration reaction, then split them back by hydrolysis.

Lab Materials & Conventions

  • Carbon (C): black
  • Nitrogen (N): red
  • Oxygen (O): blue
  • Hydrogen (H): white
  • Covalent bond: white tube

Reaction Logic

  • Dehydration reaction: joins monomers and releases water.
  • Hydrolysis: adds water to split a dimer back into monomers.

A) Dehydration Synthesis — Build a Dipeptide

Materials

  • Glycine and alanine models with amino (–NH₂) and carboxyl (–COOH) groups.
  • White covalent bond tubes; atom pieces in standard colors.

Goal

Remove H from the amino group of one amino acid and OH from the carboxyl of the other to form H₂O and connect the backbones with a new peptide bond.

Steps

What You Should See

A glycine–alanine (or alanine–glycine) dipeptide with one water molecule formed.

B) Hydrolysis — Split the Dipeptide

Goal

Add H₂O across the peptide bond to regenerate two monomers.

Steps

What You Should See

Two separate amino acids with complete amino and carboxyl groups.

C) Quick Reflection

  • How many water molecules were released/consumed?
  • Which direction did you assemble the dipeptide (N → C)?
  • Record your two‑residue sequence.
