The Tongue: An organ composed of multiple tissue types working together to provide movement, protection, and sensory input.

Let's dissect the three main tissue layers found here...

Level 3: The Tissues üî¨

The tongue is built from these three distinct tissues:

A. Epithelial Tissue: The "Covering."
Found on the surface (dark purple on slide)[cite: 31, 32].
B. Connective Tissue: The "Glue."
Found below epithelium, binds layers together[cite: 19, 20].
C. Muscle Tissue: The "Motor."
The bulk of the tongue, allows voluntary movement[cite: 22, 23].
Level 4: Cells & Molecules (The Proteins) üß™

This is where we distinguish the Builder (Cell) from the Building Material (Protein Molecule).

1. Inside Epithelial Tissue

  • The Cell: Keratinocyte (Squamous cell).
  • The Protein: Keratin (Structural)
    Provides waterproofing and protection. The top layer is non-living keratin[cite: 34, 37].

2. Inside Connective Tissue

  • The Cell: Fibroblast.
  • The Proteins:
    Collagen (Structural - Strength)
    Elastin (Structural - Flexibility)
    These form the "matrix" surrounding the cells[cite: 44, 45].

3. Inside Muscle Tissue

  • The Cell: Muscle Fiber (Cell)[cite: 50].
  • The Proteins:
    Actin (Contractile - Thin filament)
    Myosin (Contractile - Thick filament)
    These overlap to create striations and cause contraction[cite: 56, 61].
