Phase 1: Observation - Your Current Habit

Carefully read and reflect on the following observation about exam confidence.

"In my own experience, I have noticed that after exams I often feel confident about how I did, but when I receive my grade, the result does not always match that confidence. Sometimes I am surprised that my score is lower than expected, even though I believed I understood the material while studying and during the test. Other times, I feel unsure about my performance and yet still receive a reasonable grade. This mismatch between how confident I feel and how I actually perform has made me realize that judging my own learning is difficult. I can tell when I studied hard, but I am not always able to accurately evaluate whether my understanding is strong enough to lead to good exam results."

Write at least 50 words describing how the above observation mirrors or differs from your actual experience. Can you relate to the observation?

Phase 2: The Curiosity Question

What this phase is about: This phase transforms a personal observation into structured scientific curiosity. Rather than jumping to answers, you examine your own experience from multiple angles, asking why the mismatch between confidence and performance exists, how widespread it may be, and what mechanisms might explain it.

Causal Focus

  • Why does my confidence after an exam sometimes fail to match my actual score?

Population-Level Generalization

  • How accurate are students, in general, at judging their own performance before receiving feedback?

Performance-Level Differences

  • Do students who perform poorly tend to be more or less confident than they should be?

Developmental Change

  • Does experience in school help students become better at evaluating their own understanding?

Metacognitive Awareness

  • Are some students aware that their performance judgments might be inaccurate, while others are not?

Mechanisms and Cues

  • What cues do I use to decide whether I "did well" or "did poorly" on an exam?
  • Does feeling confident mean that I actually understood the material, or just that it felt familiar?

Trainability and Change Over Time

  • Can students learn to improve the accuracy of their self-judgments over time?

Conceptual Distinction

  • Is there a difference between getting the answer right and knowing that I got it right?

Role of Feedback

  • How does feedback from past exams influence how confident I feel about future exams?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (1/3) - Turn the Curiosity Question Into Research Question

What this phase is about: This phase refines broad curiosity into a precise, scientifically testable research question. You move from wondering *why* something happens to clearly defining *what exactly* will be investigated.

Research Question

Does comparing my predicted performance with my actual performance help me choose more effective study strategies?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (2/3) - Hypothesis

Hypothesis vs. Prediction:
A Hypothesis is a testable and falsifiable statement that proposes an answer to the research question. It must clearly identify the independent variable (IV)‚Äîthe factor being manipulated‚Äîand the dependent variable (DV)‚Äîthe outcome being measured.

A Prediction is the specific outcome you expect to see if the hypothesis is true (usually written as "If... then...").

Your Hypothesis

Research Question: Does systematically comparing my predicted exam scores with my actual results improve my future study decisions and performance?

Hypothesis: Systematically tracking the gap between my predicted and actual scores (IV) increases my study strategy effectiveness and academic performance (DV).

Why this works: This hypothesis is testable (we can measure if students who track predictions choose better strategies and get higher grades) and falsifiable (it could be proven wrong if tracking doesn't improve strategy selection or grades). It clearly identifies the independent variable (tracking predictions) and dependent variables (strategy effectiveness and grades).

Phase 4: Experimental Design

What this phase is about: I want to understand whether my current study strategies are fooling me. Sometimes studying feels productive even when it isn't. By tracking prediction vs. actual performance, I can see if my strategies are giving me a false sense of mastery.

The Study Design: de Bruin et al. (2020)

Students predict their quiz score, take the quiz, then compare. Over 4 quizzes, they track calibration patterns. Control group takes quizzes without predictions. All receive same study materials.

My Personal Study Design

For each quiz, I will write down my predicted overall score before starting. I will also mark my confidence for every individual question before answering it:

  • ‚úì Check mark ‚Äî I predict I will get this question correct.
  • ? Question mark ‚Äî I am unsure and cannot confidently predict.
  • üõë Stop sign ‚Äî I predict I will get this question incorrect.

After the quiz, I will compare all of my predictions‚Äîboth overall and per-question‚Äîto what actually happened. This will show me whether my study strategies are giving me a false sense of mastery or accurately preparing me.

Phase 5: The Results

What this phase is about: Here's what actually happened in the original research study (de Bruin et al., 2020) . Compare with your prediction.

Actual Study Results

Original Study Details

Citation: de Bruin, A. B., Roelle, J., Carpenter, S. K., & Baars, M. (2020). Synthesizing cognitive load and self-regulation theory. Educational Psychology Review, 32(4), 903-915.

Sample Size: n=85 undergraduate students

Effect Size: Œ∑¬≤ = 0.21 (medium to large effect)

Statistical Results

F(1,83) = 22.17, p < .001, 95% CI [4.2, 9.8]

Statistically significant (p < .05)

Data Interpretation

No calibration tracking (Control): Average grade 72, strategy consistency poor.

With calibration tracking (Experimental): Average grade 79, strategy consistency improved.

Students who tracked predictions (IV manipulation) learned to recognize when their confidence was misplaced and adjusted their study approach (DV improvement) accordingly.

Research Conclusion

The independent variable (calibration tracking) had a statistically significant effect on the dependent variables (strategy selection and grades).

Metacognitive monitoring (knowing what you know) directly improves learning outcomes when operationalized through prediction-actual comparison.
