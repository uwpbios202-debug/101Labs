Mixed Practice Wins on Delayed, Mixed Tests

Why grouped practice feels smooth but fails on real exams.

Phase 1: Observation

Carefully read, analyze, and reflect on the following.

"When I practice problems for math or science classes, I usually organize them by type because it feels more efficient. I'll do all the derivative problems first, then all the integrals, then all the chain-rule problems. It makes my homework feel smooth and predictable, like I'm on a roll. But on tests, the problems are mixed, and suddenly I freeze. I'm not always sure which strategy to use, and I end up second-guessing myself. It's frustrating because I thought I understood the material while practicing, but the test reveals I only knew how to do each type when they were grouped together. I'm starting to notice that my practice might be too tidy. I never force myself to identify which method a problem needs — I just follow the pattern in the homework. I think I've been practicing execution without practicing decision-making, and that might be why I struggle on mixed or unfamiliar problems."

Phase 2: The Curiosity Question

What this phase is about: This phase transforms a personal observation into structured scientific curiosity.

Would mixing different types of problems together during practice help me identify which strategy to use on real exams?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (1/3)

Research Question

Does interleaved practice (mixing problem types) improve problem-solving and transfer better than blocked practice?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (2/3)

The Hypothesis

Interleaved practice (IV) improves delayed and transfer performance (DV) more than blocked practice when study time is held constant (CV).

Why this works: Interleaving forces learners to discriminate between problem types and retrieve the correct strategy, creating stronger memory and better transfer.

Phase 3: Research Foundation (3/3) - Prediction

Confidence: 3/5

Phase 4: Experimental Design

The Original Study: Rohrer & Taylor (2007)

Students completed practice problems. One group practiced blocked (all of one type, then next). Another practiced interleaved (mixed types). Equal time and total problems. Performance was measured on immediate, delayed, and transfer tests.

My Personal Experiment Design

For my next math or science unit, I will create a mixed problem set combining different types of problems (e.g., derivatives, integrals, limits mixed together). Instead of doing all of one type, I'll shuffle them so I have to identify which strategy each problem requires before solving it. I'll track how this affects my ability to solve mixed problems on the actual test.

Phase 5: The Results

Here is what happened in the original study.

Original Study Details

Citation: Rohrer, D., & Taylor, K. (2007). The shuffling of mathematics problems improves learning. Instructional Science, 35(6), 481-498.

Result: Blocked practice produced 85% immediate accuracy but dropped to 62% on delayed tests. Interleaved practice started at 72% but rose to 88% on delayed tests—a 26 percentage-point advantage!

Conclusion

Hypothesis ACCEPTED. Blocked practice creates short-term fluency that fades; interleaving forces discrimination skills that improve long-term retention.
