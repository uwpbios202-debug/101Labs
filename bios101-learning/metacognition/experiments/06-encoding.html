Draw/Diagram vs Summarize vs Explain Aloud

How encoding strategy affects complex system understanding.

Phase 1: Observation

Carefully read, analyze, and reflect on the following.

"Whenever I study complex topics, I usually stick to writing summaries or rewriting my notes because it feels structured and safe. I convince myself that if I can restate the material in my own words, then I really understand it. But when I run into diagrams, processes, or multi‑step systems — like cellular respiration, the kidney, or signal pathways — I struggle to keep track of how everything connects. I can explain pieces of the system, but not how one part leads to another. I'm realizing that the way I encode information might be too flat. I rarely draw diagrams or talk through explanations out loud, even though those might help me see relationships more clearly. I think my studying focuses on describing information instead of organizing it, and that could be why these complex systems still feel confusing."

Phase 2: The Curiosity Question

What this phase is about: This phase transforms a personal observation into structured scientific curiosity.

Would drawing diagrams or explaining concepts out loud help me understand complex systems better than just writing summaries?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (1/3)

Research Question

Do different encoding strategies (visual diagrams, written summaries, verbal explanations) differentially affect comprehension and retention of complex systems?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (2/3)

The Hypothesis

Visual encoding (IV) produces better system understanding (DV) than text-based or verbal approaches when study time and materials are held constant (CV).

Why this works: Visual representations highlight spatial and causal relationships that text descriptions alone cannot capture as effectively.

Phase 3: Research Foundation (3/3) - Prediction

Confidence: 3/5

Phase 4: Experimental Design

The Original Study: Fiorella & Zhang (2016)

Students studied a complex system (e.g., photosynthesis or cellular respiration). One group created written text summaries. Another group created visual diagrams with labels. A third group explained the system verbally without notes. All groups spent equal time. Testing measured understanding of spatial relationships and causal connections within the system.

My Personal Experiment Design

I will choose a complex topic from my biology course (like the Krebs cycle or neuron signaling). Instead of just rewriting notes, I will create a visual diagram showing all components and their relationships. I will then use this diagram to explain the process out loud as if teaching someone else. I will compare this approach to my usual summary-writing method by testing my recall and understanding 48 hours later.

Phase 5: The Results

Here is what happened in the original study.

Original Study Details

Citation: Fiorella, L., & Zhang, Q. (2016). Drawing boundary conditions for learning by drawing. Educational Psychology Review, 30(4), 1115-1137.

Result: Visual diagramming produced significantly better understanding of complex systems (78%) compared to text summaries (64%) and verbal explanations (71%).

Conclusion

Hypothesis ACCEPTED. Visual encoding creates stronger mental models of complex systems by forcing organization of spatial and causal relationships that text alone doesn't capture.
