Distraction Taxes Comprehension and Note Quality

Why divided attention impairs learning effectiveness.

Phase 1: Observation

Carefully read, analyze, and reflect on the following.

"I always have something on in the background when I study—music, TV, my phone buzzing. It makes studying feel less boring, like I'm multitasking effectively. Sometimes I tell myself I'm good at handling multiple things at once, and the background noise helps me focus by blocking out other distractions. But when I'm honest, I notice my notes are incomplete, and I have to reread paragraphs multiple times. I catch myself losing track of complex explanations, missing key details, and my mind wanders more often than I'd like to admit. After a study session, I sometimes realize I spent a lot of time 'studying' but can't recall much of what I read. I'm starting to suspect that what I call 'multitasking' might actually be 'task-switching' that fragments my attention. Maybe the background stimulation is making my studying feel productive without actually helping me learn."

Phase 2: The Curiosity Question

What this phase is about: This phase transforms a personal observation into structured scientific curiosity.

How much would my comprehension improve if I studied the same material with full focus versus with my usual background distractions?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (1/3)

Research Question

Does divided attention during studying (multitasking) negatively impact learning compared to focused, single-task studying?

Phase 3: Research Foundation (2/3)

The Hypothesis

Focused attention (IV) produces better note quality and test performance (DV) than multitasking when study material and time are held constant (CV).

Why this works: Attention is a limited resource. Dividing it between study and distractions reduces encoding depth and working memory capacity for the material.

Phase 3: Research Foundation (3/3) - Prediction

Confidence: 3/5

Phase 4: Experimental Design

The Original Study: Kuznekoff & Titsworth (2013)

Students watched an educational video lecture while either focusing fully or multitasking with phones/social media. Researchers measured note-taking quality, comprehension, and retention. The multitasking group had phones available and received notifications during the lecture. Both groups had identical lecture content and duration.

My Personal Experiment Design

I will choose two similar study sessions of equal length (e.g., two 45-minute blocks). In one session, I will study with my usual distractions (phone available, music/TV). In the other session, I will put my phone in another room, close all unrelated tabs, and study in complete focus. After each session, I will immediately write down everything I remember, then compare the quality and completeness of my recall between the two conditions.

Phase 5: The Results

Here is what happened in the original study.

Original Study Details

Citation: Kuznekoff, J. H., & Titsworth, S. (2013). The impact of mobile phone usage on student learning. Communication Education, 62(3), 233-252.

Result: Focused study produced 82% comprehension vs. 64% for multitasking—an 18 percentage-point difference in learning outcomes.

Conclusion

Hypothesis ACCEPTED. Divided attention significantly impairs encoding and comprehension. The myth of multitasking is particularly harmful for complex cognitive tasks like studying.
