🔬 Amoeba (Protist) Laboratory Investigation

Pseudopodia: Movement and Food Capture in Unicellular Organisms

This investigation explores amoeboid motion—a unique form of locomotion used by protists and certain animal cells. Students will observe living amoeba demonstrating cytoplasmic streaming and pseudopodia formation, then compare these structures with similar mechanisms in human white blood cells (neutrophils).

🧬 Background Information

What is an Amoeba?

Amoeba are unicellular protists that belong to the informal group of organisms exhibiting amoeboid motion—a slow, crawling movement accomplished by extending temporary projections called pseudopodia. Unlike other protists that use flagella or cilia for locomotion, amoeba move by continuously changing their shape through cytoplasmic flow.

Protists: Complexity Within a Single Cell

Protists are eukaryotic organisms that are not plants, animals, or fungi. They represent the highest evolution of single-celled organisms and have developed considerable complexity within a single cell. What an amoeba accomplishes as a single cell—uptake and processing of nutrients, excretion of wastes, response to environmental stimuli, and reproduction—a human or other multicellular organism accomplishes through division of labor among specialized cells.

🎯 Key Principle: Amoeba demonstrate that a single eukaryotic cell can perform all life functions independently. Their cellular mechanisms provide insight into how similar processes occur in specialized cells of complex organisms.

Amoeboid Motion: How Amoeba Move

Amoeboid motion is a type of cell locomotion that results from the slow oozing of cytoplasm. The process involves:

Pseudopodia Formation

Finger-like projections ("false feet") that can bulge from anywhere on the cell surface, providing dynamic shape changes.

Cytoplasmic Streaming

Directed flow of cytoplasm into extending pseudopodia, enabling the entire cell to move in a specific direction.

Cytoskeletal Action

Microtubules and microfilaments provide structural support, while actin-myosin contractions drive movement.

Anchoring & Extension

The pseudopodium anchors its tip to a surface, then cytoplasm streams forward, pulling the cell along.

Pseudopodia: Dual Function

Pseudopodia serve two critical functions in amoeba:

1. Locomotion

Amoeba extend a pseudopodium, anchor its tip, and then stream cytoplasm into the projection. This process allows the entire cell to creep forward. Although pseudopodial action may seem chaotic, amoeba demonstrate directed movement as they creep toward food sources.

2. Food Capture (Phagocytosis)

Amoeba use pseudopodia to engulf solid food particles—typically bacteria, algae, or other protists. The process, called phagocytosis ("cell eating"), involves:

  1. Pseudopodia extend around the food particle
  2. The plasma membrane folds inward, completely surrounding the particle
  3. A food vacuole forms around the captured food
  4. The food vacuole fuses with a lysosome, whose digestive enzymes break down the food
  5. Nutrients are absorbed into the cytoplasm; waste is expelled

Key Structures in Amoeba

Nucleus

Contains the cell's genetic material (DNA + proteins = chromatin). The nucleus is clearly visible in stained amoeba but may be difficult to see in living specimens.

Food Vacuoles

Small, membrane-bound sacs containing ingested food particles. They appear as small, dark structures scattered throughout the cytoplasm.

Contractile Vacuole

A specialized organelle for osmoregulation—the control of solute concentration and water balance. Amoeba live in freshwater, which is a hypotonic environment (fewer solutes in the water compared to inside the cell). Water constantly enters the cell by osmosis. The contractile vacuole collects excess water and periodically pumps it out of the cell, preventing the cell from bursting.

💧 Osmoregulation Challenge: Freshwater protists face a constant influx of water due to osmosis. Without contractile vacuoles, these cells would swell and lyse (burst). The contractile vacuole actively expels water, maintaining cellular integrity in hypotonic environments.

Amoeba Have No Fixed Body Form

Unlike most cells, amoeba have no definite shape. They continually change form as they move and feed. This flexibility is possible because amoeba lack a cell wall (unlike plant cells) and rely entirely on their plasma membrane and cytoskeleton for structural support.

Connection to Animal Cells: Neutrophils

Certain human white blood cells called neutrophils use amoeboid motion and pseudopodia remarkably similar to those of amoeba. Neutrophils:

  • Change shape and extend pseudopodia to move through blood and tissues
  • Use phagocytosis to engulf and destroy bacteria and other foreign particles
  • Rely on actin-myosin contraction for movement
  • Form food vacuoles (phagosomes) that fuse with lysosomes to digest captured bacteria
🎯 Unity of Life Connection: The similarity between amoeba pseudopodia and neutrophil pseudopodia reveals that both protists and animals use the same fundamental cellular machinery. This shared mechanism demonstrates evolutionary conservation—the same molecular systems have been retained across vast evolutionary distances.

🧪 Method - Student Activity

Part A: Observing Living Amoeba

Step 1: Prepare the Wet Mount

  1. Locate the amoeba culture container
  2. Gently swirl the culture to distribute organisms evenly
  3. Using a clean dropper, place 2-3 drops of culture onto a clean microscope slide
  4. Important: Try to collect material from the bottom of the container where debris and amoeba tend to settle
  5. Carefully place a cover slip over the drops at a 45-degree angle to minimize air bubbles
  6. Blot excess water with a paper towel if needed
⚠️ Do NOT add stain: Staining will kill living amoeba. Observe them in their natural, unstained state.

Step 2: Initial Observation at 10X Magnification

  1. Place the prepared slide on the microscope stage
  2. Start with the 10X objective (total magnification = 100X)
  3. Use the coarse focus knob to bring the specimen into view
  4. Reduce light intensity: Use the iris diaphragm to close down the light—this increases contrast and makes transparent amoeba visible
  5. Scan the slide slowly by moving it gently on the stage
  6. Look for irregular, blob-like organisms with no definite shape
  7. Amoeba may be moving very slowly—look for cytoplasmic streaming
💡 Finding Amoeba: Amoeba are often found near debris or algae. They appear as grayish, irregularly-shaped blobs. Be patient—they move slowly and blend into the background.

Step 3: Detailed Observation - Identifying Structures

Once you locate an amoeba, observe it carefully for 3-5 minutes. Look for:

  • Pseudopodia: Finger-like projections extending from the cell body. These may be actively extending or retracting.
  • Cytoplasmic streaming: Watch the flow of cytoplasm—use arrows in your sketch to indicate direction of flow
  • Food vacuoles: Small, dark granules scattered throughout the cytoplasm (if visible)
  • Contractile vacuole: A clear, round structure that may appear and disappear as it fills with water and contracts (if visible)
  • Nucleus: Very difficult to see in living amoeba—don't worry if you can't observe it

Step 4: Document Amoeboid Motion

Watch one amoeba for at least 2-3 minutes. Ask the instructor for assistance if the amoeba is not showing clear movement.

  3. Locate a well-preserved amoeba specimen
  4. Identify the following structures:
  • Nucleus: Large, round or oval structure that stains darker than the cytoplasm. Contains chromatin (genetic material).
  • Pseudopodia: May be visible as preserved extensions from the cell body
  • Food vacuoles: Small, dark-staining circular structures containing partially digested food
  • Contractile vacuole: Larger, clear (unstained) circular structure used for water expulsion
  • Cytoplasm: The material filling the cell, containing all organelles

Part C: Observing Human Neutrophils (White Blood Cells)

Step 1: Locate the Blood Smear Demonstration Microscope

Find the demonstration microscope with the blood smear slide. This microscope uses a 100X oil immersion lens for high magnification viewing of small blood cells.

⚠️ CRITICAL: Do NOT touch the coarse focus adjustment knob. The 100X lens is extremely close to the slide. Use ONLY the fine focus knob, and only make minor adjustments.

Step 2: Identify Neutrophils

  1. Look through the eyepiece
  2. Most cells in the field are red blood cells (RBCs)—small, pink, disc-shaped cells without nuclei
  3. Look for larger cells with purple-staining, multi-lobed nuclei—these are neutrophils
  4. Neutrophils are distinguished by their lobed nucleus (looks like 2-5 connected segments)
  5. You may also see small purple fragments called platelets
🩸 About Neutrophils: Neutrophils are the most abundant type of white blood cell in humans. They patrol the bloodstream and tissues, using amoeboid motion to move toward sites of infection. When they encounter bacteria, they extend pseudopodia to engulf and destroy the invaders through phagocytosis.

Understanding Cytoplasmic Streaming

💡 What You're Seeing: Cytoplasmic streaming is the directed flow of cytoplasm within the amoeba. This flow is powered by interactions between actin filaments and myosin motor proteins—the same contractile proteins found in muscle cells. As cytoplasm flows into an extending pseudopodium, the entire cell gradually moves in that direction.

🔄 Protist-Animal Comparison: Shared Cellular Mechanisms

Pseudopodia: A Conserved Mechanism

The investigation you just completed reveals a profound example of evolutionary conservation: both protists (amoeba) and animal cells (neutrophils) use remarkably similar structures and mechanisms for movement and feeding.

Why This Similarity Matters

🎯 Evolutionary Significance: The fact that protists and animal cells use the same molecular machinery (actin, myosin, microtubules, microfilaments) for the same purposes (movement and phagocytosis) is powerful evidence that these organisms share a common evolutionary ancestor. These mechanisms evolved once in early eukaryotic history and have been conserved across hundreds of millions of years.

Key Differences: Specialization vs. Independence

Amoeba: The Complete Organism

An amoeba is a complete, independent organism. It performs all life functions as a single cell:

  • Locomotion (finding food and avoiding danger)
  • Nutrition (capturing and digesting food)
  • Osmoregulation (managing water balance with contractile vacuole)
  • Reproduction (binary fission)
  • Response to stimuli (directed movement)

Neutrophil: The Specialized Cell

A neutrophil is a specialized cell within a multicellular organism. It performs specific functions as part of the immune system:

  • Locomotion (moving through blood to infection sites)
  • Phagocytosis (destroying bacteria and pathogens)
  • Chemical signaling (recruiting other immune cells)

However, a neutrophil cannot survive independently. It depends on other body systems for:

  • Oxygen (from red blood cells and lungs)
  • Nutrients (from digestive system)
  • Waste removal (from kidneys)
  • Production (from bone marrow)
💡 Division of Labor: During evolution, as competition between unicellular organisms became intense, multicellularity and cell differentiation developed. Larger, more complex organisms had a selective advantage because they possessed different types of specialized cells that could perform specific functions efficiently. Neutrophils represent this evolutionary strategy—they excel at one task (immune defense) but depend on other specialized cells for survival.

Phagocytosis: From Feeding to Defense

The process of phagocytosis serves different purposes in different organisms:

In Amoeba (Protist):

  • Purpose: Nutrition—obtaining food for energy and growth
  • Target: Bacteria, algae, smaller protists
  • Frequency: Continuous feeding behavior
  • Outcome: Nutrients absorbed; waste expelled

In Neutrophils (Animal Cell):

  • Purpose: Defense—destroying pathogens to protect the organism
  • Target: Bacteria, fungi, cellular debris, foreign particles
  • Frequency: Activated during infection or injury
  • Outcome: Pathogen destroyed; debris cleared; neutrophil may die after engulfing many bacteria
🎯 Functional Shift: The same cellular mechanism (phagocytosis via pseudopodia) has been repurposed during evolution. In protists, it serves as a feeding strategy. In animals, it has been adapted as a defense mechanism. This demonstrates how existing cellular machinery can be co-opted for new functions during evolutionary transitions.
