🔬 Stentor (Ciliated Protist) Laboratory Investigation

Eukaryotic Cells: Exploring Dual Motility and Osmoregulation

This investigation demonstrates how a large, trumpet-shaped protist uses cilia for both locomotion and feeding, while managing water balance in a freshwater environment through specialized organelles.

📚 Curriculum Alignment

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this investigation, students will be able to:

  • Identify major structures of Stentor including cilia, macronucleus, contractile vacuole, and food vacuoles
  • Describe the dual nature of Stentor as both motile (free-swimming) and sessile (attached)
  • Observe ciliary motion and explain its dual function in locomotion and feeding
  • Compare Stentor's cilia with cilia found in animal ciliated epithelium
  • Explain the function of contractile vacuoles in osmoregulation for freshwater protists
  • Recognize the unique bead-like macronucleus structure characteristic of ciliated protists
  • Apply specialized microscopy techniques including depression slide preparation
  • Understand how living in a hypotonic environment requires specific cellular adaptations

Why Stentor is Relevant to MCAT Prep

  • Eukaryotic Cell Structure: Stentor allows students to visualize complex organelles like the contractile vacuole (relevant to osmoregulation), macronuclei, and food vacuoles. The MCAT heavily tests the differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells.
  • Cytoskeleton and Motility: Stentor uses cilia for feeding and movement. The MCAT requires students to understand the structure of cilia and flagella (9+2 microtubule arrangement) and their roles in human systems, such as the respiratory tract.
  • Homeostasis: The use of contractile vacuoles to survive in freshwater environments is a classic example of osmotic pressure and homeostasis, a core "Foundational Concept" (Concept 3) on the MCAT.

Chapter Context

Building on Previous Investigations: This Stentor investigation builds on the Euglena study by introducing a new type of locomotion structure (cilia instead of flagella) and demonstrates how protists adapt to freshwater environments through osmoregulation. Students will observe the same internal ciliary structure found in animal respiratory tissues, reinforcing the unity of eukaryotic cells.

🧬 Background Information

What is Stentor?

Stentor is a large, trumpet-shaped ciliated protist commonly found in freshwater environments. It is one of the largest unicellular organisms, reaching sizes visible to the naked eye (up to 2 mm when fully extended). Stentor demonstrates remarkable behavioral complexity for a single-celled organism.

🎯 Key Principle: Stentor is capable of BOTH motility and sessility. It can swim freely through water using its cilia, but also attaches to surfaces for extended periods. When attached, it extends into a long funnel shape and uses its cilia to create water currents that draw food particles toward its oral opening.

Stentor's Unique Characteristics

Cilia Coverage

Entire Surface: Cilia cover the entire cell surface, with especially dense concentration at the funnel-shaped anterior end

Macronucleus

Bead-Like Structure: Large nucleus resembling a string of beads, unique to ciliated protists

Contractile Vacuole

Osmoregulation: Pumps out excess water that enters by osmosis from hypotonic environment

Shape-Shifting

Variable Form: Long and funnel-shaped when attached; short and round when swimming

Cilia Structure and Function

Cilia are short, numerous, hair-like extensions that beat in coordinated waves. Like flagella, cilia contain the same "9+2" microtubule arrangement found throughout eukaryotes. In Stentor, cilia serve TWO functions:

  • Locomotion: Coordinated beating propels the cell through water when swimming
  • Feeding: Creates water currents that sweep food particles into the oral groove when attached

Living in Freshwater: The Osmoregulation Challenge

Stentor lives in freshwater, which is hypotonic (has fewer solutes compared to the protist's cytoplasm). This means water constantly moves INTO the cell by osmosis. Without a mechanism to remove this water, the cell would swell and burst.

🎯 Solution: The contractile vacuole collects excess water from the cytoplasm and periodically pumps it out of the cell. This organelle is essential for osmoregulation (control of water balance and solute concentration) in freshwater protists.

Macronucleus vs. Micronucleus

Ciliated protists like Stentor have TWO types of nuclei:

  • Macronucleus: Large, visible, bead-like structure that controls daily cell functions. This is what you will observe.
  • Micronucleus: Small, involved in reproduction (sexual conjugation). Usually not visible in living specimens.

⚙️ Preparation

Materials & Equipment

🔬 For Specimen Preparation

  • Culture of living Stentor
  • Depression slide (concave slide with well)
  • Glass cover slip
  • Disposable plastic transfer pipettes
  • Paper towels

🔬 For Microscopy

  • Dual-viewing microscope (allows instructor and student simultaneous viewing)
  • Standard light microscope (10X objective)
  • Iris diaphragm for light control

Understanding Depression Slides

🎯 Why Depression Slides? Stentor is MUCH larger than Euglena or Paramecium. A standard flat slide with coverslip would crush the organism. Depression slides have a concave well that provides vertical space, allowing Stentor to maintain its natural shape and movement without being compressed.
⚠️ Observation Challenge: Stentor is large enough to see with the naked eye but requires patience to observe well. The organism may:
  • Detach and swim away if disturbed by vibrations
  • Contract into a compact shape when stressed
  • Change position within the depression well
  • Require reduced light for optimal cilia visibility
Solution: Your instructor will prepare the slide and use the dual-viewing microscope so both you and the instructor can observe simultaneously. DO NOT bump the microscope during observation.

Safety and Handling

  • Stentor cultures should be handled gently—avoid shaking or disturbing
  • Depression slides are more fragile than flat slides—handle carefully
  • The instructor will prepare your specimen to ensure optimal viewing conditions
  • Remain still and quiet during observation to avoid disturbing attached Stentor

🧪 Method - Student Activity

Part 1: Instructor-Prepared Depression Slide

Note: Your instructor will prepare the Stentor depression slide to ensure optimal viewing conditions. This specialized preparation requires experience to prevent disturbing the organisms.

Preparation Steps (Performed by Instructor):

  1. Gently swirl the Stentor culture (do not shake)
  2. Use a transfer pipette to collect a sample with visible organisms
  3. Place 1-2 drops in the depression (well) of the slide
  4. Carefully lower a cover slip over the depression
  5. Allow organisms to settle for 1-2 minutes
  6. Place slide on dual-viewing microscope at 10X magnification

Part 2: Initial Observation with Dual-Viewing Microscope

  1. Approach the dual-viewing microscope carefully (do not bump the table)
  2. Look through your designated eyepiece while instructor observes through the other
  3. The instructor will adjust focus and locate organisms for you
  4. Observe the general shape and behavior of Stentor
  5. Note whether the organism is:
    • Attached and extended: Long, trumpet-shaped, funnel-like form
    • Swimming freely: Compact, rounded shape
⚠️ Important: If Stentor is swimming, wait patiently for it to attach to a surface. Once attached, it will extend into its characteristic trumpet shape, making structures much easier to observe. Remain completely still—any vibration may cause it to detach and swim away.

Part 3: Observing Key Structures at 10X

A. General Shape and Cilia

  1. Once Stentor is attached and extended, observe its overall shape
  2. Look for the wide, funnel-shaped anterior (top) end
  3. Focus on the edge of the organism where cilia are easiest to see
  4. Reduce light intensity using the iris diaphragm to increase contrast
  5. Look for tiny, hair-like structures beating in coordinated waves
  6. The cilia at the top (funnel opening) will be most visible and most active

B. Macronucleus

  1. Look inside the cell body for a long, chain-like structure
  2. The macronucleus appears as a string of beads or oval segments connected together
  3. It may be curved or winding through the cell body
  4. Ask the instructor for help if you cannot locate it—it can be challenging to see

C. Contractile Vacuole

  1. Look for a relatively large, clear, round structure
  2. The contractile vacuole appears empty (clear) compared to food vacuoles
  3. Watch it carefully for 30-60 seconds—you may see it:
    • Gradually increase in size (filling with water)
    • Suddenly contract and disappear (pumping water out)
    • Reappear and begin filling again
  4. This rhythmic activity is the osmoregulation process in action

D. Food Vacuoles

  1. Look for multiple small, dark structures throughout the cytoplasm
  2. Food vacuoles are smaller than the contractile vacuole
  3. They appear darker because they contain ingested food particles
  4. You may see several scattered throughout the cell

Unity of Ciliary Structure Across Eukaryotes

🎯 Critical Insight: The internal structure of cilia is IDENTICAL in Stentor (a protist), Paramecium (a protist), and the ciliated epithelial cells lining your trachea (animal tissue). All use the same 9+2 microtubule arrangement. This demonstrates that cilia evolved once in an ancient common ancestor and have been conserved throughout eukaryotic evolution.

Osmoregulation in Freshwater Environments

The Problem

Freshwater is hypotonic (fewer solutes than the protist cytoplasm). Water constantly enters the cell by osmosis.

The Solution

Contractile vacuoles collect excess water from the cytoplasm and rhythmically pump it out of the cell.

Energy Cost

This process requires ATP because water is being moved against the osmotic gradient (active transport).

Marine Protists

Marine (saltwater) protists typically lack contractile vacuoles because seawater is isotonic or slightly hypertonic to their cytoplasm.

Connecting to the Unity of Life

🎯 Chapter Integration: Your observations of Stentor demonstrate how a single-celled organism can accomplish complex tasks (movement, feeding, osmoregulation) using structures that are fundamentally similar to those found in multicellular animals. The 9+2 ciliary structure you observed in Stentor is the SAME structure found in:
  • Paramecium (another ciliated protist)
  • Ciliated epithelium in your respiratory tract (animal tissue)
  • Sperm cell flagella (animal reproductive cells)
  • Euglena flagella (flagellated protist)
This demonstrates the profound unity underlying all eukaryotic life—despite billions of years of evolutionary divergence, the same molecular machinery is conserved across kingdoms.
