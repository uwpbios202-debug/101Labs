The Surface Area Mystery

⭐ 15 Points - Apply surface area to volume principles to real cell observations

Scenario: You're a cell biologist studying why different cells have different sizes. Use what you learned about surface area to volume ratios to solve these problems.

Cell Size Data from Your Observations:

  • Onion epidermal cell: ~300-500 μm length
  • Amoeba: 200-600 μm diameter (large!)
  • Neutrophil: 10-12 μm diameter (small)
  • Red blood cell: 7-8 μm diameter (smaller)
  • Chloroplast in leaf cell: ~5 μm (tiny organelle)

Problem 1: Why Multiple Chloroplasts?

You observed that leaf parenchyma cells contain 30-40 SMALL chloroplasts instead of one HUGE chloroplast.

Problem 2: Amoeba vs. Neutrophil Size

Amoeba are 50 times larger than neutrophils, yet both use amoeboid motion.

Problem 3: Cytoplasmic Streaming Advantage

You observed cytoplasmic streaming in large plant cells and in amoeba.

      🔐 Answer Key (Instructor Only)

      Challenge 1 Answers:

      • Mystery Cell #1: Amoeba
      • Mystery Cell #2: Neutrophil (or neutrophils)
      • Mystery Cell #3: Euglena
      • Mystery Cell #4: Ciliated pseudostratified columnar epithelium (or ciliated columnar epithelium)
      • Mystery Cell #5: Stentor

      Challenge 2 Key Points:

      • Question 1: All three mechanisms (flagella, cilia, pseudopodia) appear in both protists and animal cells, demonstrating evolutionary conservation
      • Question 2: Yes, same 9+2 microtubule structure with dynein motors—shows homology (common evolutionary origin)
      • Question 3: Shows that fundamental cellular mechanisms are conserved across kingdoms; actin-myosin system evolved once and was maintained

      Challenge 3 Key Points:

      • Problem 1: Many small chloroplasts have greater total surface area than one large chloroplast, allowing faster CO₂ uptake and better light capture
      • Problem 2: Amoeba are slower-moving, have lower metabolic rate, live in environments with abundant oxygen and nutrients; cytoplasmic streaming helps transport materials
      • Problem 3: Cytoplasmic streaming reduces diffusion distances in large cells, moving materials faster than diffusion alone

      Challenge 4 Key Points:

      Organelle Found In Function
      Contractile Vacuole Paramecium, Stentor, Amoeba Osmoregulation - pumps out excess water
      Chromoplasts Carrot parenchyma Store carotene pigments (orange color)
      Macronucleus Paramecium, Stentor Controls daily cell functions
      Lobed Nucleus Neutrophils 3-5 lobes; allows cell to squeeze through tight spaces
      Cell Wall All plant cells Structural support; made of cellulose

      Critical thinking: Freshwater protists live in hypotonic environment (water enters by osmosis); neutrophils live in isotonic blood (no net water movement)

      Challenge 5 Key Points:

      • Purpose: Amoeba = nutrition/feeding; Neutrophils = immune defense
      • Engulfed: Amoeba = bacteria, algae, organic matter; Neutrophils = bacteria, pathogens
      • Mechanism: Both use actin-myosin-based pseudopodia (identical)
      • After: Both form food vacuole/phagosome, fuse with lysosome, digest contents
      • Unity of Life: Demonstrates conservation of cellular mechanisms; same molecular machinery serves different purposes in different kingdoms

      BONUS Challenge Answers:

      1. Why infections? Neutrophils normally use pseudopodia to migrate to infection sites and engulf/destroy bacteria through phagocytosis. Without functional pseudopodia, they cannot reach bacteria or perform phagocytosis effectively.
      2. Actin connection: Actin polymerizes to form microfilaments that extend pseudopodia. Myosin motors pull on actin to generate movement. Without proper actin polymerization, neutrophils cannot form pseudopodia, cannot migrate, and cannot engulf bacteria.
      3. Viral infections: May still have some problems because neutrophils participate in early antiviral response, but the main antiviral defense (NK cells, T cells, B cells) would still function. Bacterial infections would be much more severe.
      4. Microscope observation: Would see neutrophils with normal lobed nuclei BUT would NOT see pseudopodia formation or amoeboid motion. Cells would appear static/round instead of actively crawling.
