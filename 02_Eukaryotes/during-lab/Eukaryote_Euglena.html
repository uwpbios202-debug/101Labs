🔬 Euglena (Unicellular Protist) Laboratory Investigation

Domain Eukaryote | Kingdom Protista | Cell Euglena

This investigation demonstrates how a single-celled protist possesses both plant-like and animal-like features, illustrating the complexity and evolutionary relationships among eukaryotic organisms.

📚 Curriculum Alignment

Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this investigation, students will be able to:

  • Identify major structures of Euglena including chloroplasts, flagellum, nucleus, and cell membrane
  • Describe the dual plant-animal nature of Euglena as evidence of evolutionary relationships
  • Observe flagellar motion and explain its function in unicellular locomotion
  • Compare Euglena's flagellum with flagella found in animal sperm cells
  • Recognize chloroplasts as shared organelles between Euglena and plant cells
  • Apply specialized microscopy techniques including the use of methyl cellulose
  • Explain how unicellular protists accomplish life functions that multicellular organisms divide among specialized cells

🧬 Background Information

What Are Protists?

Protists are eukaryotic organisms that are not plants, animals, or fungi. They represent some of the earliest eukaryotic life forms and demonstrate remarkable complexity within a single cell.

🎯 Key Principle: What a protist accomplishes as a single cell (nutrient uptake, waste excretion, environmental response, reproduction), a human accomplishes with specialized cells working together.

What is Euglena?

Plant-Like Features

Chloroplasts: Contains numerous chloroplasts with chlorophyll for photosynthesis

Animal-Like Features

Flagellum: Long whip-like structure for locomotion, similar to animal sperm

Shared Features

Nucleus: Membrane-bound nucleus like all eukaryotes

Size

Observable: Green organisms visible at 10X; flagellum challenging even at 40X

Flagella Structure and Function

The flagellum is long, thin, and very flexible. It extends from the anterior (front) end and enables rapid swimming by whipping motion. Though difficult to observe, it demonstrates the same internal structure found in animal sperm flagella.

⚙️ Preparation

Materials & Equipment

🔬 For Specimen Preparation

  • Culture of living Euglena
  • Disposable plastic transfer pipettes
  • Glass microscope slides
  • Glass cover slips
  • Methyl cellulose solution
  • Paper towels
⚠️ Observation Challenge: Euglena's flagellum is extremely thin, transparent, and moves very rapidly. It's barely visible even at 40X. If you cannot see it, refer to lab posters, textbook diagrams (p. 601, Fig. 28.10), or ask your instructor for assistance.

Understanding Methyl Cellulose

🎯 Purpose: Methyl cellulose increases viscosity to slow protist movement. Add it AFTER initial observations. Observe immediately—it works quickly. The goal is to slow movement, not stop it completely.

🧪 Method - Student Activity

Part 1: Initial Wet Mount Preparation

  1. Gently swirl the Euglena culture (do not shake vigorously)
  2. Use a transfer pipette to place TWO drops of culture on a clean slide
  3. Lower a cover slip at 45° angle to minimize air bubbles
  4. Blot excess liquid with paper towel

Part 2: Initial Observation at 4X

  1. Place slide on microscope stage
  2. Start with 4X objective lens (lowest magnification)
  3. Use coarse focus to locate organisms
  4. Small green organisms should be swimming rapidly
  5. Adjust the iris diaphragm to make the field as dark as possible (increases contrast)
  6. Rotate the revolving nose piece to the 10X Always use ONLY the fine focus knob at 10X—never the coarse focus to bring the cells into view

Labels to include: Chloroplasts, General cell shape, Direction of movement (use arrow)

Your 10X sketch of swimming Euglena

Part 3: Attempting to Observe the Flagellum at 40X

  1. Switch to 40X objective (use ONLY fine focus)
  2. Critical: Reduce light intensity using iris diaphragm—this increases contrast
  3. Look carefully at the anterior (pointed) end of Euglena
  4. Watch for a thin, transparent, rapidly whipping structure
  5. Be patient—this requires careful observation

Part 4: Using Methyl Cellulose to Slow Movement

  1. Locate the methyl cellulose near the culture
  2. Lift the cover slip carefully
  3. Add ONE drop of methyl cellulose to your slide
  4. Dry the cover slip and replace it
  5. Observe immediately—organisms slow down quickly
  6. Try again to observe the flagellum at 40X with reduced light
Note: If organisms stop moving completely, prepare a fresh slide. Completely immobile Euglena won't show flagellar movement.

👁️ Observation & Results

What Students Should Observe

At 10X Magnification

  • Green color: Numerous chloroplasts give Euglena its characteristic green appearance
  • Rapid swimming: Organisms move quickly through the field of view
  • Elongated shape: Oval or spindle-shaped body
  • Directional movement: Swimming in specific directions, not just drifting

At 40X Magnification

  • Chloroplasts: Individual green organelles distributed throughout cytoplasm
  • Anterior end: More pointed end where flagellum emerges
  • Flagellum (if visible): Thin, whip-like structure extending from anterior end
  • Nucleus: Very difficult to see in living specimens without staining

Expected Results

✓ Successful observation includes:
  • Clear identification of green, swimming organisms
  • Visible chloroplasts at 40X magnification
  • Recognition of anterior-posterior orientation
  • Observation of directed swimming motion
  • Attempt to observe flagellum (success varies)

Troubleshooting

Problem Likely Cause Solution
Cannot see any organisms Sample taken from top of culture or organisms settled Mix culture gently; sample from middle depth
Organisms moving too fast Normal behavior for Euglena Add methyl cellulose; reduce light for better contrast
Cannot see flagellum Flagellum is transparent and moves rapidly Reduce light with iris diaphragm; use 40X; be patient; refer to reference materials
Organisms stopped moving Too much methyl cellulose or organisms stressed Prepare fresh slide with culture
Green color too pale Insufficient light or culture needs nutrients Increase light slightly; check culture health with instructor

🔍 Comparative Analysis: Unity Across Kingdoms

Part A: Euglena's Plant-Like Characteristics

🌱 Question 1: What structure does Euglena share with plant cells (like the onion cels you observed previously)?

Your Answer:

Expected Answer: Euglena contains chloroplasts with chlorophyll, just like plant leaf cells. Both use these organelles for photosynthesis to produce energy from sunlight. This demonstrates that chloroplasts evolved before the divergence of plant and protist lineages.

Part B: Euglena's Animal-Like Characteristics

🏃 Question 2: What structure does Euglena use for locomotion, and which animal cells have you observed (or will observe) that use the same type of structure?

Your Answer:

Expected Answer: Euglena uses a flagellum for locomotion. Animal sperm cells also use flagella to move through the female reproductive tract toward the egg cell. The flagellum in Euglena is attached at the anterior (front) end, while in sperm cells it's typically attached at the posterior (rear) end. Despite this difference in attachment location, the internal structure of both flagella is identical, demonstrating shared cellular machinery across kingdoms.

Part C: Unity Across Kingdoms

🔬 Question 3: Based on your observations, explain how Euglena demonstrates that protists represent an evolutionary link between plants and animals.

Your Answer:

Expected Answer: Euglena demonstrates evolutionary connections because it possesses characteristics of BOTH plants and animals in a single cell. Like plants, Euglena has chloroplasts and performs photosynthesis to produce energy from sunlight. Like animals, Euglena has a flagellum for active locomotion and can move toward favorable conditions. This combination of features suggests that plants, animals, and protists share common evolutionary ancestors, and that these different kingdoms diverged from organisms that had both capabilities.

Part E: Flagellar Structure Comparison

🔬 Deep Dive: The internal structure of flagella is remarkably conserved across eukaryotes. Both Euglena flagella and animal sperm flagella contain the same "9+2" microtubule arrangement: nine pairs of microtubules arranged in a circle around two central microtubules. This identical internal architecture is powerful evidence that all eukaryotes share a common ancestor.
🧠 Question 4: You observed that Euglena's flagellum is located at the anterior (front) end, while sperm flagella are located at the posterior (rear) end. Despite this difference in location, both serve the same function. What does this tell you about evolution?

Your Answer:

Expected Answer: This shows that evolution modifies existing structures for new purposes rather than creating entirely new structures from scratch. Both Euglena and sperm cells inherited the basic flagellar machinery from a common ancestor, but evolution adapted the placement and orientation to suit each organism's specific needs. Euglena pulls itself forward with anterior flagella, while sperm push themselves forward with posterior flagella. The underlying mechanism (the 9+2 microtubule structure) remains the same, demonstrating evolutionary conservation of successful designs.

Part F: Completing the Life Functions Comparison

🌱 Question 5: The lab manual states: "What a protist accomplishes as a single cell, a human accomplishes with specialized cells working together." Based on your observations of Euglena, explain what this means.

Your Answer:

Expected Answer: Euglena must perform ALL life functions within a single cell: it uses chloroplasts to make food through photosynthesis, uses its flagellum to move toward light, takes in nutrients from the environment, excretes wastes, responds to stimuli (like light), and reproduces. In contrast, a human has specialized cells for each function: specialized photoreceptor cells for sensing light, muscle cells for movement, digestive cells for processing nutrients, kidney cells for waste removal, and reproductive cells for reproduction. Euglena demonstrates that a single cell can be remarkably complex and perform all the functions necessary for life.
