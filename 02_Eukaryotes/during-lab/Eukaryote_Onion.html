🧬 Background Information

What is a Cell?

A cell is the smallest unit that can carry out all life activities. Cells provide strong evidence for the underlying unity of all living things. When we examine a wide range of seemingly diverse organisms—from unicellular protists to the most complex plants and animals—we find striking similarities at the cellular level. The unity of life is evident in the universal genetic code shared by all organisms.

Eukaryotic vs. Prokaryotic Cells

The defining feature of a eukaryotic cell is the presence of a membrane-bound nucleus that contains the cell's DNA. Plant cells, animal cells, and protist cells are all eukaryotic. In contrast, prokaryotic cells (bacteria and archaea) lack a nucleus and have their DNA floating freely in the nucleoid region.

Plant Cell Structure

Plant cells are eukaryotic cells that have additional structures not found in animal cells:

Cell Wall

A rigid outer layer made of cellulose that surrounds the plasma membrane, providing support and protection.

Plastids

Membrane-bound organelles for synthesis and storage, including Chloroplasts (photosynthesis), Amyloplasts (starch storage), and Chromoplasts (pigments).

Central Vacuole

A large, fluid-filled organelle that maintains turgor pressure and stores nutrients or waste products.

Nitroplast (Deep Dive – Not Observed)

A recently discovered organelle (2024) found in some algae that enables nitrogen fixation.

Important: Nitroplasts are not observable in onion cells or in this lab. This example is included to show that cell biology is an evolving field and that new organelles are still being discovered.

Why Study the Onion Cell?

The onion epidermal cell is ideal for this investigation because:

  • Plant cells are large enough to observe clearly under light microscopy
  • The nucleus is prominent and easily visualized with staining
  • Onion cells are non-photosynthetic, making it easier to see nuclear structures without chloroplast interference
  • The cell wall is visible and clearly demonstrates the difference between plant and animal cells
  • Results serve as a template for understanding all plant cells in subsequent investigations
🎯 Key Principle: Although onion epidermal cells are specialized and non-photosynthetic, they contain all the fundamental structures shared by ALL eukaryotic plant cells. By mastering this investigation, you create a framework for understanding other cell types.

The Unity of Life Connection

As you examine onion cells, you'll notice they contain structures identical to those found in animal cells and protist cells: a nucleus, nuclear membrane, chromatin, and mitochondria. This demonstrates that despite the diversity of life forms, all eukaryotic organisms are united by fundamental cellular similarities.

Preparing the Culture

Note: Prepare fresh cell samples immediately before observation.

Preparing Your Workstation

Step 1: Organize Your Space

  • Arrange materials in a logical order on your lab bench
  • Place microscope at a comfortable height with good lighting
  • Keep paper towels readily accessible
  • Designate the side bench as slide preparation area

Step 2: Clean Your Microscope

  • Use lens paper (never paper towels) to clean objective lenses
  • Clean the eyepiece last
  • Wipe the stage clean
  • Confirm the 4X objective lens is clicked into place directly above the light opening before viewing
  • Adjust the iris diaphragm to full open position
  • Raise the stage to its highest position to allow proper initial focusing at low power

Step 3: Pre-Lab Safety Review

  • Stains: Avoid contact with skin; wash hands if exposure occurs
  • Microscope: Do not apply excessive pressure to slides; never use the coarse focus at 10X or 40X
  • Glass: Report any broken slides or cover slips to instructor immediately
⚠️ Safety Reminder: The objective lenses are valuable and easily damaged. Do not let the lens touch the slide except through proper focusing procedures. Always start with 4X magnification and only move to 40X after checking focus at lower magnification.

🧪 Method - Student Activity

Wet Mount Slide Preparation

Step 1: Extract the Onion Epidermis

  1. Select a small onion piece (about 2-3 cm square)
  2. Using forceps, grasp the outer papery skin layer
  3. Gently peel off the transparent inner layer from the curved surface (this is the epidermis)
  4. Place the thin, transparent layer on a clean, dry microscope slide
  5. Important: Avoid wrinkles and folds in the tissue—this will make viewing difficult
Student sketch: How to extract onion epidermis with forceps (10X magnification guide)

Step 2: Add Stain

  1. Using a dropper, place 1-2 drops of stain solution directly onto the onion epidermis
  2. Do NOT add water first—stain is applied to moist tissue
  3. Wait 3 minutes for stain to penetrate and make nuclei visible
  4. Note: Excess stain can be blotted with a paper towel if color is too dark

Step 3: Add Cover Slip

  1. At a 45-degree angle, carefully lower a cover slip onto the stained tissue
  2. Lower it gradually to avoid trapping large air bubbles
  3. If major air bubbles are present, lift the cover slip and try again
  4. Small air bubbles are acceptable and will not interfere with observation

Step 4: Blot Excess Stain

  1. Use a paper towel to gently blot excess stain from the edges of the cover slip
  2. This improves visibility and prevents staining your microscope

Microscopy Observation Procedure

At 4X Magnification

  1. Place the prepared slide on the microscope stage
  2. Start with the 4X objective (lowest magnification)
  3. Use the coarse focus knob to bring cells into view
  4. Adjust the iris diaphragm to make the field as dark as possible (increases contrast)
  5. Locate a region with clear, intact cells
  6. Identify the overall cell shape and cell wall outline
  7. Locate the cell nucleus (will appear as a darker region)
  8. Rotate the revolving nose piece to the 10X Always use ONLY the fine focus knob at 10X—never the coarse focus to bring the cells into view
  9. Adjust the iris diaphragm prepare to sketch 3-5 adjacent cells with labels

10X Magnification Sketch

Labels to include: Cell wall (outline shape of one cell), Plasma membrane (inside the cell wall), Nucleus

Your 10X magnification sketch of onion epidermal cells

At 40X Magnification

  1. Slowly turn the revolving nose piece to the 40X objective
  2. Important: Always use ONLY the fine focus knob at 40X—never the coarse focus
  3. Adjust focus until cell structures become sharp and clear
  4. Reduce light intensity using the iris diaphragm to increase contrast
  5. Examine the nucleus closely—look for:
    • Nuclear membrane: The boundary of the nucleus
    • Chromatin: Dark-staining threads or granules inside the nucleus
    • Nucleolus: One or more small, round structures INSIDE the nucleus
  6. Examine the cytoplasm (area outside the nucleus but inside the plasma membrane)
  7. Make a detailed sketch of a single cell showing internal structures

👁️ Observation & Results

What Students Should Observe

At 10X Magnification

  • Cell walls: Clear boundaries forming rectangular (or polygonal) shapes
  • Entire cells: Each cell appears as a distinct rectangular unit
  • Nuclei: Darker-staining spherical or oval structures, typically located near the cell center
  • Multiple cells: Observe at least 5-10 intact cells in your viewing field
  • Cell arrangement: Cells fit together like puzzle pieces—demonstrating they are closely packed in tissue

At 40X Magnification

  • Nuclear membrane: A distinct boundary separating the nucleus from the cytoplasm
  • Chromatin: Fine threads or granular material distributed throughout the nucleus (appears dark after staining)
  • Nucleolus: One or more small, round, very dark-staining regions WITHIN the nucleus (these are ribosome-producing sites)
  • Cytoplasm: The material surrounding the nucleus, appearing lighter than the nucleus
  • Cell wall and plasma membrane: Visible as distinct layers at the cell periphery

Expected Results

✓ If your preparation was successful, you should observe:
  • Clear rectangular cells arranged in rows
  • Well-stained nuclei that are distinctly darker than the cytoplasm
  • Visible nucleoli within the nucleus (may appear as 1-3 small dots)
  • Good optical clarity with minimal air bubbles
  • Sharp focus at both 10X and 40X magnification

Troubleshooting

Problem Likely Cause Solution
Cells appear too pale Insufficient stain or stain not yet penetrated Add 1-2 more drops of stain; wait additional 2 minutes
Cells appear too dark (cannot see detail) Too much stain or cell tissue too thick Blot excess stain; prepare new slide with thinner tissue layer
Image is blurry at 40X Objective lens not properly focused or dirty Use ONLY fine focus knob at 40X; clean lens with lens paper
Large air bubbles present Cover slip lowered too quickly Prepare new slide; lower cover slip slowly at 45-degree angle
Nucleus not visible Tissue may be from outer epidermal layer (lacks nucleus) or insufficient staining Use inner epidermis layer; increase staining time
Wrinkles in tissue Tissue layer too thick or forceps handled roughly Prepare new slide; handle tissue gently with forceps

Answering Analysis Questions

Question 1: What is chromatin?

Chromatin is made of DNA + proteins (called histones). It appears as dark-staining threads or granules within the nucleus and contains the cell's genetic instructions.

Question 2: What is the nucleolus?

The nucleolus is a region WITHIN the nucleus where ribosomal RNA (rRNA) is synthesized. It appears as a very small, round, intensely dark-staining structure inside the nucleus.
