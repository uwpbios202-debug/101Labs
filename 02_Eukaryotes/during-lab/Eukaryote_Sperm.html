🧬 Background Information

What are Flagella?

Flagella (singular: flagellum) are long, whip-like cellular appendages used for locomotion. They are structurally similar to cilia but are distinguished by being longer and fewer in number. If a cell has only one or a few long appendages, they are called flagella; if the appendages are short and numerous, they are called cilia.

The 9+2 Microtubule Arrangement

All eukaryotic flagella share an identical internal structure consisting of microtubules arranged in a specific pattern:

  • 9 outer doublets: Nine pairs of microtubules arranged in a circle
  • 2 central singlets: Two single microtubules in the center
  • Dynein arms: Motor proteins that cause sliding of microtubules, generating the whip-like motion
  • ATP requirement: Flagellar movement requires energy from ATP
🎯 Key Principle: The 9+2 microtubule arrangement is universally conserved across all eukaryotic flagella and cilia—from protists to plants to animals. This structural identity demonstrates that these organelles evolved once in early eukaryotic history and have been maintained for over a billion years.

Euglena: A Protist with Dual Characteristics

Euglena is a fascinating unicellular protist that exhibits characteristics of both plants and animals:

Plant-Like Features

Contains chloroplasts with chlorophyll pigment, enabling photosynthesis in the presence of light.

Animal-Like Features

Possesses a long flagellum for rapid locomotion and can consume organic matter heterotrophically in darkness.

Anterior Flagellum

The flagellum is attached at the anterior (front) end and pulls the cell forward through water.

Rapid Movement

Euglena swim very quickly, making observation of the flagellum challenging without special techniques.

Key Structures in Euglena

Flagellum

A long, thin, whip-like structure extending from the anterior end. It rapidly whips back and forth, propelling the cell forward. The flagellum is extremely thin and flexible, making it difficult to observe even at 400X magnification.

Chloroplasts

Numerous green organelles containing chlorophyll, the photosynthetic pigment. These give Euglena their characteristic green color and enable them to produce their own food from sunlight.

Nucleus

Contains the cell's genetic material but is very difficult to see in living Euglena without staining.

Eyespot (Stigma)

A light-sensitive organelle that helps Euglena detect light and swim toward it (phototaxis). This enables them to find optimal light conditions for photosynthesis.

Bull Sperm: Animal Cells with Flagella

Mammalian sperm cells use flagella for locomotion through the female reproductive tract. Key features include:

Posterior Flagellum

Unlike Euglena, sperm have their flagellum attached at the posterior (rear) end, pushing the cell forward rather than pulling it.

Head

Contains the nucleus with tightly packed DNA. In prepared slides, the head appears as a distinct oval structure.

Midpiece

Contains mitochondria that produce ATP to power flagellar movement.

Tail (Flagellum)

A long flagellum with the same 9+2 microtubule structure as Euglena, but oriented posteriorly.

💡 Functional Similarity: Despite the difference in orientation (anterior vs. posterior), both Euglena flagella and sperm flagella serve the same basic function: propelling the cell through a fluid environment. Both require ATP, both use the 9+2 microtubule structure, and both employ dynein motor proteins to generate movement.

Why Flagella are Difficult to Observe

Observing flagella presents several challenges:

  • Extreme thinness: Flagella are only about 0.2 micrometers in diameter—near the resolution limit of light microscopy
  • Rapid movement: Flagella beat hundreds of times per second, creating a blur
  • Transparency: Flagella are transparent and don't stain well in living specimens
  • Speed of the organism: Euglena swim so fast that tracking individual cells is difficult
⚠️ Observation Tip: To improve your chances of seeing flagella, use the iris diaphragm to reduce light intensity (increasing contrast) and be patient. You may only catch brief glimpses of the flagellum as it whips by. Using methyl cellulose to slow down the organisms can help, but timing is critical—too much slowing stops movement entirely.

Preparing the Culture

Note: The instructor will maintain live Euglena cultures. These cultures contain Euglena living in pond water or culture medium with nutrients. Do not add stains or fixatives to living Euglena—this will kill them and stop flagellar movement.

Understanding Methyl Cellulose

Methyl cellulose is a thickening agent that increases the viscosity of the culture medium, slowing down protist movement without killing the organisms. However, timing is critical:

  • Too little: Organisms still move too fast to observe
  • Just right: Organisms slow enough to see flagellar movement
  • Too much/too long: Organisms stop moving completely
⚠️ Critical Timing: After adding methyl cellulose, observe the slide IMMEDIATELY. The slowing effect increases rapidly, and you may only have 2-3 minutes of optimal viewing time before movement stops completely. If you miss this window, prepare a fresh slide.

🧪 Method - Student Activity

Part A: Observing Living Euglena WITHOUT Slowing Agent

Step 1: Prepare the Initial Wet Mount

  1. Locate the Euglena culture container
  2. Gently swirl the culture to distribute organisms evenly
  3. Using a clean dropper, place 2-3 drops of culture onto a clean microscope slide
  4. Carefully place a cover slip over the drops at a 45-degree angle to minimize air bubbles
  5. Blot excess water with a paper towel if needed
⚠️ Do NOT add methyl cellulose yet: First observe the natural speed of Euglena movement to appreciate how fast they swim.

Step 2: Initial Observation at 10X Magnification

  1. Place the prepared slide on the microscope stage
  2. Start with the 10X objective (total magnification = 100X)
  3. Use the coarse focus knob to bring the specimen into view
  4. Reduce light intensity: Use the iris diaphragm to close down the light—this increases contrast
  5. Scan the slide by moving it gently on the stage
  6. Look for small green organisms swimming rapidly across the field
  7. Notice how quickly they move—they appear as green blurs darting across your view
💡 What You're Seeing: Euglena typically swim in a spiral pattern, rotating as they move forward. Their small size and rapid movement make them appear as tiny green dots or streaks zipping through the water. This demonstrates why we need a slowing agent to observe their flagella.

Part B: Observing Euglena WITH Methyl Cellulose

Step 1: Add the Slowing Agent

  1. Carefully lift one edge of the cover slip using forceps or your fingernail
  2. Add ONE drop of methyl cellulose to the edge of the cover slip
  3. The methyl cellulose will flow under the cover slip by capillary action
  4. Gently lower the cover slip back down
  5. Blot any excess liquid with a paper towel
  6. OBSERVE IMMEDIATELY—you have only 2-3 minutes of optimal viewing time

Step 2: Observe Slowed Euglena at 10X

  1. Return the slide to the microscope stage
  2. Focus at 10X magnification
  3. Reduce light with the iris diaphragm for maximum contrast
  4. Watch for Euglena that are still moving but at a slower pace
  5. Select one organism that is moving slowly enough to track
  6. Observe its overall shape and green color from chloroplasts

Step 3: Attempt to Observe the Flagellum at 40X

  1. Center a slowly-moving Euglena in your field of view at 10X
  2. Carefully rotate to the 40X objective
  3. Use ONLY the fine focus knob to sharpen the image
  4. Close the iris diaphragm significantly to reduce light and increase contrast
  5. Look at the more pointed anterior (front) end of the Euglena
  6. Watch for a thin, whip-like structure extending from the anterior end
  7. You may only see brief glimpses as the flagellum rapidly whips about
  8. Be patient—observing the flagellum is challenging even under ideal conditions
📝 Observation Tip: The flagellum appears as a very thin, flexible thread that whips back and forth rapidly. It may be easier to see when the Euglena is moving slowly or momentarily pauses. Don't be discouraged if you can't see it clearly—even experienced microscopists find flagella difficult to observe in living cells.

Step 4: Identify Other Structures

While observing at 40X, look for these additional structures:

  • Chloroplasts: Numerous small green organelles distributed throughout the cell
  • Cell shape: Euglena are elongated and somewhat spindle-shaped
  • Anterior end: The more pointed end where the flagellum emerges
  • Eyespot (if visible): A small red-orange spot near the anterior end

Understanding Flagellar Function

💡 What You're Seeing: When a flagellum beats, it creates a wave-like motion that propagates from the base to the tip. This whip-like action generates thrust, propelling the cell through water. The flagellum doesn't simply rotate like a propeller—it undulates in a complex three-dimensional pattern. This movement requires ATP and is powered by dynein motor proteins that cause microtubules within the flagellum to slide past each other.

🔄 Protist-Animal Comparison: Shared Locomotion Structures

Flagella: A Universally Conserved Structure

The investigation you just completed reveals another profound example of evolutionary conservation: both protists (Euglena) and animal reproductive cells (sperm) use structurally identical flagella for locomotion.

Why This Similarity Matters

🎯 Evolutionary Significance: The structural identity of flagella in Euglena and bull sperm—both using the exact same 9+2 microtubule arrangement, both powered by dynein motor proteins, both requiring ATP—is compelling evidence that these structures evolved once in an ancient common ancestor and have been conserved for over a billion years. Despite the enormous differences between a free-living photosynthetic protist and a highly specialized mammalian reproductive cell, they share fundamental cellular machinery.

The 9+2 Pattern: A Universal Blueprint

The 9+2 microtubule arrangement is found in:

  • Protist flagella (Euglena, Chlamydomonas, dinoflagellates)
  • Animal sperm flagella (all animals, from insects to mammals)
  • Cilia in animal tissues (respiratory tract, oviducts)
  • Protist cilia (Paramecium, Stentor)
  • Even some plant cells (sperm of mosses and ferns)
💡 Prokaryotic Flagella Are Different: It's important to note that bacterial flagella are completely different structures with a completely different mechanism. Bacterial flagella rotate like propellers and are made of the protein flagellin, not microtubules. Eukaryotic flagella evolved separately and use a fundamentally different design. This investigation focuses only on eukaryotic flagella.

Key Differences: Orientation and Purpose

Euglena: Anterior Flagellum

Euglena have their flagellum attached at the anterior (front) end:

  • Pulling motion: The flagellum extends forward and pulls the cell through water
  • Purpose: General locomotion for finding optimal light conditions (phototaxis)
  • Flexibility: Can change direction readily to respond to environmental cues
  • Context: Free-living organism that must navigate complex aquatic environments

Bull Sperm: Posterior Flagellum

Sperm cells have their flagellum attached at the posterior (rear) end:

  • Pushing motion: The flagellum propels the cell forward from behind
  • Purpose: Directional swimming through reproductive tract to reach egg
  • Streamlined: Head-first orientation reduces drag during forward swimming
  • Context: Highly specialized cell with one specific mission—fertilization
💡 Functional Adaptation: The different orientations (anterior vs. posterior) represent adaptations to different functional requirements. Euglena need maneuverability to explore their environment, while sperm need efficient forward propulsion through a specific medium. Despite this difference, the underlying molecular machinery is identical—demonstrating how the same structure can be oriented differently to serve varied functions.

Euglena: Plant-Like AND Animal-Like

Euglena are fascinating organisms that blur the traditional boundaries between plant and animal kingdoms:

Plant-Like Characteristics:

  • Chloroplasts: Contain chlorophyll and perform photosynthesis
  • Autotrophic capability: Can produce own food from sunlight
  • Green coloration: Due to chlorophyll content

Animal-Like Characteristics:

  • Flagellum: Active locomotion structure
  • Heterotrophic capability: Can consume organic matter when light is unavailable
  • Lack of cell wall: Flexible pellicle instead (like animal cell membrane)
  • Contractile vacuole: For osmoregulation (like other protists)
🎯 Evolutionary Lesson: Euglena demonstrate that the traditional categories of "plant" and "animal" are human constructs that don't always fit natural organisms neatly. Protists like Euglena possess characteristics of both kingdoms, reminding us that evolution doesn't follow our classification systems—organisms evolve whatever features help them survive.
