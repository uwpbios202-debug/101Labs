Step 1 – Listen→Deconstruct→Synthesize→Speak

One word at a time, you've got this!

Eukaryotic
Morpheme breakdown: Eu- (true) + karyo- (nucleus) + -tic (relating to)

Cells with a true, membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.

Prokaryotic
Morpheme breakdown: Pro- (before) + karyo- (nucleus) + -tic (relating to)

Cells that lack a membrane-bound nucleus (before nucleus). DNA floats freely in the cytoplasm.

Organelle
Morpheme breakdown: Organ (functional structure) + -elle (small/diminutive)

Specialized structure with a specific function within a cell (little organ).

Chloroplast
Morpheme breakdown: Chloro- (green) + -plast (formed, molded)

Photosynthetic organelle containing chlorophyll (green pigment).

Chromoplast
Morpheme breakdown: Chromo- (color) + -plast (formed, molded)

Pigment-storing plastid responsible for colors in fruits and flowers.

Leucoplast
Morpheme breakdown: Leuco- (white, colorless) + -plast (formed, molded)

Colorless storage plastid for starch, lipids, or proteins.

Mitochondrion
Morpheme breakdown: Mito- (thread) + chondrion (granule)

ATP-producing organelle, the "powerhouse" of the cell.

Endoplasmic Reticulum
Morpheme breakdown: Endo- (inside) + plasm (formed substance) + reticulum (network)

Network of membranes inside the cytoplasm for protein and lipid synthesis.

Cytoskeleton
Morpheme breakdown: Cyto- (cell) + skeleton (framework)

Structural protein network that maintains cell shape and enables movement.

Flagellum
Morpheme breakdown: From Latin "flagellum" meaning whip

Long, whip-like locomotor structure for cell propulsion.

Photosynthesis
Morpheme breakdown: Photo- (light) + synthesis (putting together)

Process of converting light energy into chemical energy (sugars).

Nucleus
Morpheme breakdown: Nucle- (kernel, nut) + -us (noun ending)

The central organelle containing genetic material; the "core" of the cell.

Chromatin
Morpheme breakdown: Chromat- (color) + -in (chemical substance)

The material of which chromosomes are composed (DNA and proteins); named because it stains easily with dyes.

Nucleolus
Morpheme breakdown: Nucle- (kernel) + -olus (diminutive/little)

A small, dense structure within the nucleus (the "little nucleus") where ribosomes are made.

Plasma membrane
Morpheme breakdown: Plasma (something molded/formed) + Membrana (skin, film)

The outer boundary of the cell that molds the cell's contents and acts as a skin.

Cytoplasm
Morpheme breakdown: Cyto- (cell) + -plasm (something molded/formed)

The jelly-like material within a cell that holds the organelles.

Plastid
Morpheme breakdown: Plast- (molded, formed) + -id (small structure/particle)

A major organelle found in plants and algae, responsible for manufacturing and storing food.

Amyloplast
Morpheme breakdown: Amylo- (starch) + -plast (molded/formed)

A type of plastid specifically responsible for storing starch.

Nitroplast
Morpheme breakdown: Nitro- (nitrogen) + -plast (molded/formed)

A recently discovered organelle (evolved from a bacterium) that fixes nitrogen within a cell.

Pigment
Morpheme breakdown: Pingere (to paint) + -ment (result of action)

A substance that gives color to plant or animal tissues.

Chlorophyll
Morpheme breakdown: Chloro- (green) + -phyll (leaf)

The green pigment found in the leaves of plants.

Carotene
Morpheme breakdown: Carota (carrot) + -ene (chemical suffix)

An orange or red pigment (first isolated from carrots).

Cilia
Morpheme breakdown: Cilia (eyelash)

Short, microscopic, hairlike vibrating structures (resembling eyelashes) that provide propulsion.

Pseudopodia
Morpheme breakdown: Pseudo- (false) + -podia (feet)

Temporary protrusions of the surface of an amoeboid cell for movement and feeding ("false feet").

Vacuole
Morpheme breakdown: Vacu- (empty) + -ole (diminutive/small)

A space or vesicle within the cytoplasm, often containing fluid (appearing "empty" under early microscopes).

Osmoregulation
Morpheme breakdown: Osmo- (pushing, impulse) + -regulation (rule, order)

The maintenance of constant osmotic pressure (water balance) in the fluids of an organism.

Phagocytosis
Morpheme breakdown: Phago- (eat) + -cyt- (cell) + -osis (process/condition)

The process by which a cell engulfs or "eats" a solid particle.

Ribosome
Morpheme breakdown: Ribo- (from Ribose/RNA) + -soma (body)

A minute particle ("body") consisting of RNA and associated proteins, functioning to synthesize proteins.

Lysosome
Morpheme breakdown: Lyso- (loosen, dissolve) + -soma (body)

An organelle containing digestive enzymes causing the "dissolution" or breakdown of waste.

Tonoplast
Morpheme breakdown: Tono- (tension, stretching) + -plast (molded/formed)

The membrane surrounding the central vacuole, maintaining turgor pressure (tension) in plant cells.

Macronucleus
Morpheme breakdown: Macro- (large) + -nucleus (kernel)

The larger type of nucleus in ciliates (protists), which controls non-reproductive cell functions.

Protist
Morpheme breakdown: Protist- (the very first)

A eukaryotic organism that is not an animal, plant, or fungus (often considered the first eukaryotes).

Epithelium
Morpheme breakdown: Epi- (upon) + -thele (nipple) + -ium (tissue structure)

Tissue forming the outer layer of a body's surface.

Neutrophil
Morpheme breakdown: Neutro- (neither/neutral) + -phil (loving)

A type of white blood cell that stains easily with neutral dyes (loving neither acid nor base exclusively).

Leukocyte
Morpheme breakdown: Leuko- (white) + -cyte (cell)

A colorless cell that circulates in the blood (White Blood Cell).

Parenchyma
Morpheme breakdown: Para- (beside) + -en- (in) + -chyma (something poured)

The functional tissue of an organ ("poured in beside" the structural framework).

Homeostasis
Morpheme breakdown: Homeo- (same, similar) + stasis (standing still)

Maintenance of stable internal conditions despite external changes.

Step 2 – First Exposure - Plain Reading - Big Picture

1st, Is this hierarchy familiar to you?

Eukarya → Kingdom (Protista, Plantae, Animalia) → Tissue → Cell → Organelle → Molecules

Please casually read

In Lab 1 you learned that eukaryotic DNA is contained within a membrane-bound nucleus. This nuclear organization is just the beginning of eukaryotic complexity. In Lab 2 you'll explore the diverse organelles and structures that different eukaryotic cells possess. You'll examine plant cells with specialized plastids—chloroplasts containing chlorophyll in Elodea leaf cells and chromoplasts containing carotene in carrot root cells. You'll observe unicellular protists that use different structures for movement: Euglena with its whip-like flagellum, Paramecium covered with thousands of beating cilia, and Amoeba extending flowing pseudopodia to crawl and capture food. Remarkably, these same structures appear in multicellular organisms—flagella propel sperm cells, cilia line ciliated pseudostratified columnar epithelium in your respiratory tract moving mucus upward, and pseudopodia extend from neutrophil white blood cells to engulf bacteria. Despite their diversity, eukaryotic cells across protists, plants, and animals use the same fundamental structures, revealing the underlying unity of life.
