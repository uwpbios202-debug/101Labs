🔍 STEP 1: Emily's Observation

🎯 Goal Identification - Testable vs Untestable Claims

What am I trying to master in this step?

🧪 STEP 2: Practice - Identifying Claims

Before we continue with Emily's experiment, let's practice identifying whether claims are testable or not. Click each claim to see the answer.

Claim 1: "I can sense your energy field with my hands." 🔽

Is this testable? Yes.

Why or why not? You can design a blinded hand-detection test (as JAMA did for Therapeutic Touch) where the practitioner must identify the location of an unseen hand above theirs. If an energy field is perceptible, performance should exceed chance; in the JAMA study, practitioners performed at chance levels (≈44%), failing to detect any "field."

Claim 2: "Happiness is the most important thing in life." 🔽

Is this testable? No.

Why or why not? This is a normative/value statement (what should matter most). It doesn't specify an observable, falsifiable condition, a measurable variable, or a criterion for refutation. It's about values, not empirical observation—so it isn't testable in the scientific sense.

Claim 3: "Meditating with crystals reduces anxiety more than meditating with visually identical placebo stones." 🔽

Is this testable? Yes.

Why or why not? You can run a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled study comparing anxiety outcomes (e.g., Beck Anxiety Inventory) between real-crystal and placebo-stone groups. Recent controlled work finds no specific effect beyond placebo—improvements occur only among believers, regardless of whether the stone is real or placebo.

Claim 4: "Astrological signs determine personality traits." 🔽

Is this testable? Yes.

Why or why not? You can correlate zodiac signs with validated personality measures (e.g., Big Five inventories) across large samples. Findings show no scientifically valid linkage; belief in astrology correlates with higher narcissism and lower measured intelligence, not with stable trait profiles by sign.

Claim 5: "Homeopathic remedies work because water 'remembers' substances once diluted away." 🔽

Is this testable? Yes.

Why or why not? The water-memory mechanism can be tested by seeing whether serially diluted solutions (beyond Avogadro's limit) retain biological effects. The famous Benveniste report (1988) sparked tests, but replications under control have failed; the claim contradicts established physical chemistry and lacks reproducible evidence.

📋 STEP 4: Forming a Hypothesis

🤔 What Makes a Good Hypothesis?

A hypothesis is a testable, falsifiable prediction about the relationship between variables.

  • Testable: You can design an experiment to see if it's true
  • Falsifiable: Data could potentially show it's wrong
  • Specific: Clearly states how IV affects DV
💭 Emily's Hypothesis:

"If Therapeutic Touch practitioners truly can sense a human energy field (HEF) emanating from a person's body, then—under blinded conditions where they cannot see any cues—they should be able to detect, at rates significantly above chance (>50%), which of their hands is closest to the 'energy source' (the volunteer's unseen hand)."

If practitioners score above 50%, the hypothesis is supported. If they score at or below 50%, it's not. This is what makes it both testable and falsifiable.
❌ Not quite - Option A: A good hypothesis doesn't assume the answer. It makes a prediction that could go either way based on the data.
❌ Not quite - Option C: A hypothesis isn't "guaranteed" to prove anything. It's a testable prediction that the data will either support or fail to support.
❌ Not quite - Option D: Emily's strength was focusing on the simple, fundamental claim rather than complex healing outcomes.

🎯 Goal Identification - Experimental Design

What am I trying to master in this step?

🛠️ Emily's Experimental Setup

The Setup (performed in Emily's home):

  1. Emily sat across from a TT practitioner with a tall cardboard screen between them
  2. The practitioner put both hands, palms up, through two holes in the screen
  3. Emily used a coin flip to randomly select which of the practitioner's hands she would hold her hand above (about 3-4 inches away, without touching)
  4. The practitioner had to identify which of their hands was near Emily's hand
  5. This was repeated multiple times per practitioner
🎲 Key Design Element: Coin flip randomization ensured no patterns Emily could unconsciously create

🔬 STEP 5: Why This Design Works

Question: What does the cardboard screen (blinding) accomplish in this experiment?

AIt makes the experiment look more official and scientific
BIt prevents Emily from cheating by giving hints to practitioners
CIt eliminates visual cues (like Emily's breathing, posture, or shadows) that could allow practitioners to guess hand position rather than sense energy
DIt keeps the practitioners from seeing how young Emily is
✅ Perfect understanding of experimental controls! The screen isolates the claimed ability from confounding sensory information. If practitioners could see Emily, they might unconsciously detect visual cues (shadows, air movement, body language) and mistake those for "energy field" detection. The blind design ensures that only the claimed energy-sensing ability is being tested.
❌ Not quite - Option A: The screen serves a critical scientific purpose, not an aesthetic one. It's an essential control that removes confounding variables.
❌ Not quite - Option B: While blinding does prevent signaling, the primary purpose is to eliminate ordinary sensory cues that the practitioners might use, consciously or unconsciously, instead of "energy sensing."
❌ Not quite - Option D: Emily's age wasn't the concern. The screen's purpose was to eliminate all visual information that could substitute for the claimed energy-sensing ability.

🎲 The Role of Randomization

Follow-up Question: Why did Emily use a coin flip to decide which hand to place hers above?

This is the gold standard for experimental design!

❌ Not quite - Option C: The coin flip added minimal time but provided critical methodological validity.
❌ Not quite - Option D: Coin flips aren't about tradition—they're a simple, effective method for achieving true randomization.

🔬 Final Page: A Case Study in Therapeutic Touch

1. Etymology of "Therapeutic Touch"

The practice combines two linguistic elements:

  • Therapeutic: From Greek therapeutikos (θεραπευτικός), meaning "healing" or "attending to the sick"
  • Touch: From Old French touche, meaning "to touch"

Important note: Despite the name, Therapeutic Touch practitioners typically hover their hands above the body without physical contact. The "touch" refers to interacting with the claimed "Human Energy Field" rather than physical touch.

Related term - Human Energy Field (HEF): A concept borrowed from various spiritual traditions claiming humans emit or are surrounded by an energetic aura that can be manipulated for healing.

2. Why is Therapeutic Touch Considered Pseudoscience?

Therapeutic Touch meets the criteria for pseudoscience because it:

  • Uses scientific-sounding language ("energy field," "bioplasmic energy") without scientific methodology
  • Makes testable claims that fail under controlled testing (as demonstrated by Emily Rosa's experiment)
  • Relies on subjective validation rather than objective evidence
  • Lacks a plausible mechanism consistent with established physics or biology
  • Doesn't self-correct in response to negative evidence
  • Claims are unfalsifiable when practitioners offer excuses for failures

3. The Fundamental Reason We Consider Therapeutic Touch Pseudoscience

It fails the most basic test of a claimed sensory ability:

When tested under controlled conditions that eliminate ordinary sensory cues:

  • Rosa (1998): Practitioners performed at 44% accuracy (worse than 50% chance)
  • Follow-up studies: Multiple replications have confirmed these findings
  • Meta-analyses: Systematic reviews find no evidence for HEF detection
  • Clinical trials: While some show subjective improvement, these are indistinguishable from placebo effects

💡 The Core Issue: Therapeutic Touch claims to involve a detectable, manipulable energy field, but practitioners cannot demonstrate this ability under controlled conditions. The subjective benefits reported are explained by known psychological mechanisms (placebo, relaxation, attention) rather than the claimed energy manipulation.

4. Moving the Goalposts in Therapeutic Touch

When faced with negative evidence like Emily Rosa's study, Therapeutic Touch defenses often involve "moving the goalposts":

Claim When Tested Response When Fails Scientific Problem
"We can detect human energy fields" "The test conditions were unnatural" or "Emily's energy was too weak" Makes claim untestable; if it only works under unspecified "natural" conditions, it's not a reliable ability
"The energy field is a physical phenomenon" "It's a spiritual/energetic reality that science can't measure" Abandons empirical claim while keeping scientific trappings; becomes unfalsifiable
"Practitioners need client interaction" "Blinded tests eliminate the energetic connection" If the ability requires knowing the client's identity or condition, it suggests cues rather than energy detection
"Our training produces reliable practitioners" "Only some practitioners are truly gifted" Special pleading; no objective criteria for distinguishing "gifted" from non-gifted practitioners

🎯 The Scientific Standard: A valid sensory ability should work under controlled conditions that eliminate ordinary cues. If Therapeutic Touch only "works" when practitioners can see the patient, sense body heat, or receive verbal feedback, then it's those ordinary cues—not energy field detection—producing the results.

🏁 Conclusion: Therapeutic Touch as a Case Study in Medical Pseudoscience

Therapeutic Touch provides a perfect case study in medical pseudoscience because:

  1. It makes testable empirical claims (energy field detection) that fail under controlled testing
  2. It uses scientific terminology while rejecting scientific methodology
  3. It maintains institutional support (hospitals, nursing schools) despite negative evidence
  4. It demonstrates classic pseudoscientific defenses (goalpost-moving, special pleading)
  5. It confuses mechanism with outcome (benefits from human interaction ≠ energy manipulation)

The takeaway for healthcare: We should value compassionate human interaction in medicine while being honest about mechanisms. The therapeutic alliance, attentive presence, and caring touch have demonstrated benefits—but these don't require inventing undetectable energy fields. Evidence-based medicine requires separating genuine therapeutic elements from pseudoscientific explanations.
